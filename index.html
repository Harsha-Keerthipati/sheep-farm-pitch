The Farm

One Structure, Three Operations

Sheep on top, open air in the middle, chickens below — all draining to one shared floor. This single idea is what makes the economics work.

2.1 · The Three-Layer Integrated Shed

Cross-section — the most important diagram on this page.

Two separated waste streams — nothing falls on the birds. 318 sheep produce 382 litres of urine a day plus dry pellets. Under the sheep slats sits a slat-on-slat catchment deck, pitched from the centre down to both side walls: the dry pellets ride on an open top slat layer and slide off into a side trench → composting bays, while urine falls through onto a smooth sealed pan just below and drains to a separate side pipe → liquid fertiliser for the ponds (a wedge-wire screen at the wall makes the final liquid/solid split). Because both layers are open, gravity does the sorting — no mechanical scraper, no moving parts to fail — and chicken-floor air rises straight through, so the birds sit sealed clean and dry below, draining only their own droppings to the PCC.

Build phasing & cost: the catchment deck is installed per shed when the chickens move in — not in Batch 1, which runs sheep-only with waste dropping to the ground floor. Gravity-driven, so no scraping labour and no scraper to maintain. Costed at ≈ ₹4.5–5.0 L/shed — slatted frame + sealed pan + dual side gutters + wedge-wire screens; the eliminated scraper roughly offsets the added gutters (estimate — confirm with a fabricator quote).

Chicken flooring: poultry-grade plastic slats (~22 mm gap, anti-slip) — feet rest on the bars while droppings drop through; not bare wire, so no toe entrapment. A dry slat floor keeps feet cleaner than wet litter (the main cause of footpad lesions); the trade-off is reduced litter-foraging behaviour. Feed is served from feeders, not the floor. Costed at the existing ~₹91/sqft all-in, which already covers plastic-slat-grade flooring — no CapEx change.

Ventilation & biosecurity: the sheds stay open-sided for natural cross-ventilation; circulation (assist) fans — one per pen, at both decks — sweep fresh air across the animals and out the open sides on still, hot days, with an open ridge letting heat and ammonia rise away. Wall exhaust fans short-circuit in an open shed, so the design uses circulation fans, not tunnel ventilation — which also keeps the solar load modest. Two species under one roof is the real risk to manage: all-in/all-out batches, full vaccination, and separate exhaust per deck.

Side netting: the open sides carry shade cloth + roll-up curtains (sun + wind-driven monsoon rain), with finer insect mesh only where pests are a proven problem — ≈ ₹65 k/shed.

A dedicated 1,500 sq ft insulated brick room in the NW corner. Day-old chicks need 32–35 °C — provided by 6 infrared brooding lamps for 14 days. One bay covers the chicken ramp-up; a second bay (NE biosecurity corner, ≈ ₹2.5 L) is added once all four sheds run broilers — at full rotation a single bay can't restock a shed every ~12 days.

A written medical timeline the caretaker follows for every batch.

  7. D60

How it's funded — and when you get paid back

The owner seeds Shed 1 (~₹20.8L) from personal savings — the only out-of-pocket. After that, 100% of revenue and profit is reinvested into the next shed and the next batch's animals; no profit is drawn until Acre 3 is running. By then the operation throws off enough to fund the remaining acres and begin repaying the capital from the start. So an investor is an accelerator, not a gap-filler: their capital lets the wholesale acres go up sooner instead of waiting for cash to pile up.

Why Batch 1 Loses Money and Batch 3 Doesn't — Explained

The near-breakeven in Batches 1–2 has nothing to do with inexperience or mistakes. It is structural — caused by two unavoidable constraints that both resolve at Batch 3.

1

Feed cost — the main driver

  • Batches 1–2: commercial TMR at ₹26.40/animal/day (₹2,640/animal/batch).
  • Batch 3+: self-made HF + concentrate + roughage at ₹17.00/animal/day (₹1,700/animal).
  • Saves ₹940/animal — ₹2.35 Lakhs on 250 lambs per batch.
  • Can't switch sooner: (a) the HF system isn't built in Batch 1; (b) the formula must be confirmed on real animals in the Batch 2 trial before committing 250 lambs to it.
2

Scale — fixed costs need dilution

  • Fixed costs (vet visits, caretaker, biosecurity, contingency) ≈ ₹1.38L whether you have 100 or 250 lambs.
  • At 100 lambs: ₹1,385/lamb. At 250 lambs: ₹554/lamb.
  • Scale adds ₹2.07 Lakhs to Batch 3 vs Batch 1.
  • Starting at 100 is deliberate risk management: a Batch 1 failure risks ₹7L in procurement — at 250 it would be ₹17.5L.
Combined improvement, Batch 1 → Batch 3 (feed + scale alone) +₹4.4 Lakhs

At festival prices (₹420/kg), Batch 3 returns ~₹1,600–1,800 per surviving lamb — consistent with what AP sheep fatteners report. At average live-sale prices (₹360/kg) without restaurant wholesale, Batch 3 is still only near breakeven. This is why restaurant-wholesale relationships must be established before Batch 3 exits — not just for higher margin, but to eliminate festival-timing dependency entirely.

Growth Plan

Prove One Acre, Then Replicate It

4.1 · The Build in Four Stages

Milestone-driven, not calendar-driven — each stage begins only once the previous one is proven and has funded it. With every acre proven before the next, full build-out lands in the late 2030s.

1

Prove

Acre 1, shed by shed. Shed 1 is the trial; once full (~300) it funds Shed 2, then 3, then 4, then the chicken floors. Owner seeds Shed 1; all revenue reinvested.

2

Replicate

Acre 2 the same way; then Acre 3. Once Acre 1 works, Acre 2 is built shed by shed too. Once both acres run full for a batch, Acre 3 goes up wholesale.

3

Self-sustaining + payback

Acre 3 running. Profit now funds Acres 4 & 5 together and begins repaying the owner's input — the first profit drawdown.

4

Full scale

5 acres. 6,360 sheep + 60,000 chickens, own feed, processing, solar, year-round supply to three cities — ~₹800L/year.

Two species under one roof (ammonia, cross-disease)

Medium

The slat-on-slat deck keeps sheep waste off the birds; open sides + circulation fans + an open ridge clear ammonia rather than letting it build. All-in/all-out batches, full vaccination on both species, and separate exhaust per deck contain cross-contamination.

Hydroponic fodder underperforms

Medium

Post-Batch-3 margins lean on self-made feed, so it's proven in the Batch-2 trial before 250 lambs depend on it. If it stumbles, the farm falls back to commercial TMR — feed cost rises but the operation continues.

The PPR risk — honest statement

PPR mortality in unvaccinated flocks ranges 10–100%. At 1,272 lambs × ₹7,000, a catastrophic event is significant. The vaccine works reliably with an unbroken cold chain. A monthly vet consultant from Batch 1 (₹5,000–8,000/visit) is the single most important non-capital investment in this business.

Water risk

Rayalaseema is drought-prone, so water is treated as a first-order risk: a standby borewell per acre from the start, two recharge ponds (49-day full-farm buffer), and storage sized to peak rather than average demand. (See the risk row above.)

Hard rules — written protocol, not memory

  • Only maize, barley, cowpea, and horse gram permitted as HF seeds. Pearl millet and sorghum sprouted leaves contain prussic acid — toxic to sheep.
  • Any confirmed disease in a chicken batch → full litter cleanout and shed sanitisation before the next batch. No exceptions.
  • ET vaccine booster at Day 21–28 is mandatory — the primary dose alone gives only partial immunity.
