The Farm

One Structure, Three Operations

Sheep on top, open air in the middle, chickens below — all draining to one shared floor. This single idea is what makes the economics work.

2.1 · The Three-Layer Integrated Shed

Cross-section — the most important diagram on this page.

Two separate waste streams — nothing falls on the birds. 318 sheep produce 382 litres of urine a day plus dry pellets. A catchment deck under the sheep slats — a hollow, sloped box with a perforated top — catches both: urine drops through the holes into the box and runs down-slope to a side gutter and sump (becoming liquid fertiliser for the ponds), while the dry pellets ride on top and a mechanical scraper clears them once a day to the composting bays. The chickens sit sealed below that deck on their own slats, draining only their own droppings to the PCC. The two never meet over the birds — that is what keeps the lower deck dry and ammonia-free.

Build phasing & running cost: the catchment deck + daily scraper are installed per shed when the chickens move in (Batches 5–8) — not in Batch 1, which runs sheep-only with waste dropping to the ground floor. The daily mechanical scraper means no added scraping labour; power draw is negligible (solar-covered), plus periodic blade/cable maintenance. Costed at ≈ ₹5.0 L/shed — perforated deck + frame + urine channel ≈ ₹3.75 L, mechanical daily scraper ≈ ₹1.25 L (estimate; confirm with a fabricator / automation quote).

Chicken flooring: poultry-grade plastic slats (~22 mm gap, anti-slip) — feet rest on the bars while droppings drop through; not bare wire, so no toe entrapment. A dry slat floor keeps feet cleaner than wet litter (the main cause of footpad lesions); the trade-off is reduced litter-foraging behaviour. Feed is served from feeders, not the floor. Costed at the existing ~₹91/sqft all-in, which already covers plastic-slat-grade flooring — no CapEx change.

A dedicated 1,500 sq ft insulated brick room in the NW corner. Day-old chicks need 32–35 °C — provided by 6 infrared brooding lamps for 14 days. Purebred Nellore (Jodipi / Brown strain) — the premium meat breed of AP, sourced from Rajampet and Proddatur shandies. 2.5 · The 100-Day Sheep Protocol

A written medical timeline the caretaker follows for every batch.

  1. D1

    Arrival + quarantine

    Electrolytes, vitamins. No TMR for 24 hrs. Dry straw for rumen rebalancing.

  2. D3

    Parasite clearance

    Individual weighing. Albendazole + Ivermectin. 5-day liver tonic.

  3. D7

    ET vaccine (primary)

    Enterotoxemia 2 ml SC, before the high-grain diet begins.

  4. D15

    PPR + ecto-spray

    PPR vaccine 1 ml SC. Amitraz spray — all ticks, lice, mites.

  5. D20

    Slatted deck migration

    Main elevated deck. 800 g/day split AM/PM. Ammonium Chloride 0.5%.

  6. D21–28

    ET booster (mandatory)

    Without this, the primary ET vaccination is only partial protection.

  7. D60

    Second deworming

    Closantel (different class — resistance prevention). Feed up to 1.2 kg/day.

  8. D95–100

    Final weighing + exit

    Pre-booked buyers. Kanuma day = festival price premium.

Between every batch (Days 101–110): manure scrape → pressure wash → Formalin spray → white lime → 7-day dry fallow. No animal enters until complete.

Why Batch 1 Loses Money and Batch 3 Doesn't — Explained

The near-breakeven in Batches 1–2 has nothing to do with inexperience or mistakes. It is structural — caused by two unavoidable constraints that both resolve at Batch 3.

1

Feed cost — the main driver

  • Batches 1–2: commercial TMR at ₹26.40/animal/day (₹2,640/animal/batch).
  • Batch 3+: self-made HF + concentrate at ₹14.72/animal/day (₹1,472/animal).
  • Saves ₹1,168/animal — ₹2.92 Lakhs on 250 lambs per batch.
  • Can't switch sooner: (a) the HF system isn't built in Batch 1; (b) the formula must be confirmed on real animals in the Batch 2 trial before committing 250 lambs to it.
2

Scale — fixed costs need dilution

  • Fixed costs (vet visits, caretaker, biosecurity, contingency) ≈ ₹1.38L whether you have 100 or 250 lambs.
  • At 100 lambs: ₹1,385/lamb. At 250 lambs: ₹554/lamb.
  • Scale adds ₹2.07 Lakhs to Batch 3 vs Batch 1.
  • Starting at 100 is deliberate risk management: a Batch 1 failure risks ₹7L in procurement — at 250 it would be ₹17.5L.
Combined improvement, Batch 1 → Batch 3 (feed + scale alone) +₹5.0 Lakhs

At festival prices (₹420/kg), Batch 3 returns ₹1,800–2,000 per surviving lamb — consistent with what AP sheep fatteners report. At average live-sale prices (₹360/kg) without restaurant wholesale, Batch 3 is still only near breakeven. This is why restaurant-wholesale relationships must be established before Batch 3 exits — not just for higher margin, but to eliminate festival-timing dependency entirely.

What this changes

  • Restaurants receive mutton every month, year-round, on supply contracts.

The PPR risk — honest statement

PPR mortality in unvaccinated flocks ranges 10–100%. At 1,272 lambs × ₹7,000, a catastrophic event is significant. The vaccine works reliably with an unbroken cold chain. A monthly vet consultant from Batch 1 (₹5,000–8,000/visit) is the single most important non-capital investment in this business.

Water risk

One borewell per acre. If it fails, two farm ponds provide 49 days of full 5-acre buffer while alternatives are arranged. A secondary borewell per acre (Acres 3–5) is a ₹2.0L contingency that can be added as the operation scales.

Hard rules — written protocol, not memory

  • Only maize, barley, cowpea, and horse gram permitted as HF seeds. Pearl millet and sorghum sprouted leaves contain prussic acid — toxic to sheep.
  • Any confirmed disease in a chicken batch → full litter cleanout and shed sanitisation before the next batch. No exceptions.
  • ET vaccine booster at Day 21–28 is mandatory — the primary dose alone gives only partial immunity.
